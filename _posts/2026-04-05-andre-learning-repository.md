*This article documents the experience of designing and building a learning activity repository for a young student named André — a project that unexpectedly taught me as much as it aimed to teach him. Written partly as a reflection, partly as a response to questions from his tutors.*

---

## How it started

It began simply enough: a family member needed structured learning support, and the traditional options weren't quite fitting. André is curious, energetic, and responds well to challenge — but tends to disengage when content feels abstract or patronizing. The usual worksheets weren't working.

I offered to put something together. What I didn't anticipate was how seriously I'd end up taking it.

## What I built

The repository is a collection of learning activities — primarily focused on mathematics, with ratio, proportion, and number sense as core themes. Each activity is designed to feel like a challenge rather than an exercise: a problem to solve, not a concept to absorb.

The design philosophy has a few deliberate principles:

**Challenge first, explanation later.** The character Blanca — a recurring guide in the activities — doesn't lecture at the start. She responds *after* an attempt, whether the answer is right or wrong. This mirrors how good tutoring works: you learn more from engaging with a problem than from hearing about it.

**Language as a variable.** Activities are written in Spanish, André's working language, with care taken not to lose nuance in translation. This turned out to be more complex than expected — mathematical language has idioms, and some phrases that work in English are awkward or misleading in Spanish.

**Progressive difficulty without ceiling anxiety.** Each activity has an entry point accessible to André's current level, but doesn't signal where the "hard" version begins. The goal is to remove the anxiety of "this is too advanced for me" before he even starts.

## What I learned

A few things surprised me:

**Designing for a real learner is humbling.** I had to constantly revise my assumptions about what "clear" means. What reads as obvious to me can be genuinely confusing without the background knowledge I take for granted.

**Pedagogy and system design are more similar than they look.** The pattern of "bounded progressive clarification" I used in the activities — give context, let the learner attempt, provide feedback at the right depth — is structurally identical to how I think about conversational AI agent design. Good interfaces, whether human or machine, don't front-load all their intelligence.

**Consistency matters more than cleverness.** The most useful thing I built wasn't the most inventive activity — it was a simple, consistent format that André could navigate without having to re-learn the interface every time.

## Responses to questions from the tutors

When I shared the repository with André's tutors, they asked several good questions. Here's my attempt to answer them directly:

**"How do you ensure the difficulty calibration is appropriate?"**

Honestly: iteration and observation. I built starting conditions based on what André had shown he could do, then adjusted after seeing where he got stuck and where he breezed through. There's no substitute for watching a real learner work through something.

**"What's the plan for expanding the content?"**

The repository is designed to grow incrementally — one activity at a time, each following the same structure. The consistent format means new activities don't require onboarding. The backlog is roughly thematic: once ratio and proportion feel solid, the plan is to move toward data interpretation and basic algebraic thinking.

**"How do you handle incorrect answers in a way that doesn't discourage?"**

This was the design challenge I spent the most time on. Blanca's responses to incorrect answers are carefully written to acknowledge the attempt, identify the specific misunderstanding (not the student), and offer a concrete next step — not just "try again." The tone is consistent: direct but not cold, encouraging but not hollow.

## What I'd do differently

If I were starting over, I'd invest earlier in a shared format spec — a template that makes authoring new activities faster and more consistent. I built the first few activities freehand, and had to retrofit consistency afterward.

I'd also build in a lighter feedback mechanism with the tutors from the start. The best improvements to the repository came from their observations, and getting that input earlier would have accelerated the useful iterations.

---

## A note on this project

This isn't a product. It's a personal project built for one kid, by someone who cares about him learning well. If any of this is useful to others in a similar position, that's a bonus — but the measure of success is simpler: does André engage with it, and does he understand more afterward?

So far, the answer to both is mostly yes.
